[Intro] Song opening

The [Intro] tag opens your song and sets the mood. It's the first thing the AI generates, so it defines the initial atmosphere and instrumentation. Think of it as the curtain-raiser — it should grab attention and establish the sonic palette for what's to come.

Recommended Lines

2–4 lines

Placement

Always at the very beginning of the song

Tips

  • Use atmospheric or scene-setting lyrics rather than narrative
  • Keep it short — long intros can cause the AI to lose direction
  • Instrumental-only intros work well; just include the tag with no lyrics beneath

Example

[Intro]
City lights flicker in the rain
A lonely heart beats in the dark
[Verse 1] & [Verse 2] Narrative progression

[Verse 1] and [Verse 2] carry the narrative of your song. [Verse 1] introduces the story, theme, or emotional core. [Verse 2] should add depth, a different perspective, or advance the storyline — never simply repeat the first verse's sentiment. The contrast between verses creates narrative momentum.

Recommended Lines

4–6 lines each

Placement

After [Intro], alternating with [Chorus]

Tips

  • Keep verses roughly the same length for structural consistency
  • Number them explicitly — [Verse 1], [Verse 2] — not just [Verse]
  • Verse 2 should feel like a natural evolution, not a copy of Verse 1
  • You can add a [Verse 3] for longer songs, but two verses is the sweet spot

Example

[Verse 1]
I remember the way you smiled
Summer days that lasted miles
We were young and we were wild
Running free like untamed child

[Verse 2]
But seasons change and people fade
The photographs begin to gray
I trace the lines upon the page
A different me, a different age
[Pre-Chorus] Tension builder

The [Pre-Chorus] is your tension-building section — the ramp before the drop. It sits between the verse and the chorus, cranking up emotional intensity so the chorus hits with maximum impact. Not every song needs one, but it adds professional polish when used correctly.

Recommended Lines

2–4 lines

Placement

Between [Verse] and [Chorus]

Tips

  • Increase emotional intensity with each line
  • Often uses rising melodic phrases that lead into the chorus
  • Never use [Pre-Chorus] without a following [Chorus]
  • Can repeat the same lyrics before both Verse 1 and Verse 2 choruses

Example

[Pre-Chorus]
And I feel the walls closing in
Like the ending's about to begin
[Chorus] The main hook

The [Chorus] is the heart of your song — the section listeners remember and sing along to. Suno AI gives extra musical emphasis to content within [Chorus] tags, making this the most emotionally charged and sonically rich part of the track. Put your strongest, most memorable lyrics here.

Recommended Lines

4–6 lines

Placement

After [Verse 1], [Verse 2], and often before [Outro]

Tips

  • Repeat the chorus at least twice — once is never enough
  • Use simple, punchy language that's easy to remember
  • The title or central theme of the song should appear in the chorus
  • Avoid making the chorus too long (8+ lines) — it dilutes impact
  • Use identical lyrics for all chorus repetitions; Suno handles musical variation

Example

[Chorus]
We are the fire burning bright
Dancing shadows in the night
We are the echo of the fight
Rising higher, taking flight
[Bridge] Contrast and relief

The [Bridge] breaks the repetition of verse-chorus cycles. It introduces a new musical or lyrical idea — a moment of reflection, revelation, or contrast. The bridge should feel different from everything that came before it, offering the listener a fresh perspective before the final chorus or outro.

Recommended Lines

4 lines

Placement

After the second [Chorus], before the final [Chorus] or [Outro]

Tips

  • A different melodic feel is encouraged — let the AI surprise you
  • Use lyrical content that reframes or deepens the song's meaning
  • A bridge without a following chorus can work for ballads but is unusual
  • Keep it concise; 4 lines is the sweet spot

Example

[Bridge]
Maybe we were never meant to stay
Just passing ships along the way
But in the silence I still say
I'd do it all again today
[Guitar Solo] Instrumental break

The [Guitar Solo] tag instructs Suno AI and Udio to generate an instrumental guitar segment at that position. This is a standalone section — you should not include any lyrics beneath it. The AI will create a melodic guitar break that fits the song's key and style.

Recommended Lines

0 lines of lyrics (standalone tag only)

Placement

Between [Verse 2] and [Chorus] for maximum impact

Tips

  • Never write lyrics under [Guitar Solo] — it's purely instrumental
  • Works best in rock, metal, synthwave, and pop-rock genres
  • Pair with genre tags like "Electric Guitar Solo" in your style prompt for stronger results
  • Don't place it at the very beginning — it confuses the AI
  • One [Guitar Solo] per song is enough; multiple solos can feel excessive

Example

[Verse 2]
The city sleeps beneath the gray
Another night, another day

[Guitar Solo]

[Chorus]
We are the fire burning bright...
[Vocal Break] A cappella / vocal interlude

The [Vocal Break] tag creates a vocal-focused interlude, often with minimal or no instrumental backing. This is perfect for emotional peaks, a cappella moments, or showcasing vocal prowess. Use it sparingly for dramatic effect — it's a powerful tool when positioned correctly.

Recommended Lines

2–4 lines

Placement

After [Chorus] or [Bridge], before final [Chorus]

Tips

  • Best for emotional peaks — place it where the song needs a breath
  • Works well in ballads, pop, R&B, and soul genres
  • Use minimal style prompt instrument tags when using [Vocal Break]
  • Avoid placing it adjacent to [Guitar Solo]; choose one or the other

Example

[Vocal Break]
(Ooh, I'm falling)
(Can you hear me calling?)
(Way across the night)
[Outro] The ending

The [Outro] is the closing section of your song. It provides resolution — whether that's a fade-out, a final lyrical statement, or an instrumental wind-down. Never skip this tag. Songs without an outro often sound abruptly cut off or incomplete, which significantly degrades perceived quality.

Recommended Lines

2–4 lines

Placement

Always at the very end of the song

Tips

  • Always include [Outro] — it's the most commonly skipped tag and the #1 cause of incomplete-sounding songs
  • Fade-out style works well: softer, shorter lines that trail off
  • Can echo a key line from the chorus for thematic closure
  • Instrumental-only outros work fine — just the tag with no lyrics

Example

[Outro]
The lights go dim, the music fades
Just memories of better days
I walk alone into the haze

Tag Combinations That Work

These proven tag sequences produce consistently well-structured songs. Choose the template that fits your genre and song length.

Standard Pop/Rock Structure

[Intro] → [Verse 1] → [Chorus] → [Verse 2] → [Chorus] → [Bridge] → [Chorus] → [Outro]

The most versatile template. Works for pop, rock, indie, country, and R&B.

Extended Rock/Metal Structure

[Intro] → [Verse 1] → [Pre-Chorus] → [Chorus] → [Verse 2] → [Pre-Chorus] → [Chorus] → [Guitar Solo] → [Bridge] → [Chorus] → [Outro]

Full-featured structure with solo and pre-chorus. Ideal for rock, metal, and synthwave.

Vocal-Centric Ballad Structure

[Intro] → [Verse 1] → [Chorus] → [Verse 2] → [Chorus] → [Vocal Break] → [Chorus] → [Outro]

Showcases vocals with a dedicated break. Best for ballads, pop, soul, and R&B.

Minimal Lo-Fi Structure

[Intro] → [Verse 1] → [Chorus] → [Verse 2] → [Chorus] → [Outro]

Short and sweet. Perfect for lo-fi, chill, and ambient tracks where simplicity is key.

Best Practices

1

Always start with [Intro] and end with [Outro]

These bookend tags give the AI clear boundaries. Without them, songs feel shapeless or cut off.

2

Repeat the chorus at least twice

Songs with only one chorus feel incomplete and underdeveloped. Two choruses is the minimum for a satisfying structure.

3

Keep sections consistently sized

Don't make one verse 8 lines and another 2. Consistent section lengths help the AI maintain a coherent musical flow.

4

Use optional tags sparingly

[Guitar Solo] and [Vocal Break] add professional polish but aren't needed in every song. One or two optional tags per song is plenty.

5

Use parentheses ( ) for backing vocals

Wrapping lyrics in parentheses tells Suno and Udio to layer them as harmonies or background vocals behind the main vocal line.

6

Put your best lyrics in the chorus

Suno AI gives extra emphasis to chorus content. The chorus is where your song's identity lives — make every line count.

Common Mistakes

Forgetting to close sections

Every [Verse] needs an explicit end before the next tag begins. Don't let lyrics from one section bleed into another without a tag boundary.

Over-stuffing the chorus

A chorus with 8+ lines loses its punch. The AI struggles to give equal weight to every line, and the section becomes musically diluted.

Using [Pre-Chorus] without a following [Chorus]

The pre-chorus exists solely to build into a chorus. Without one, it creates unresolved tension that makes the song feel broken.

Placing [Guitar Solo] at the very beginning

Starting with a guitar solo confuses the AI about the song's structure. Solos belong in the middle or late sections, never as openers.

Using parentheses incorrectly for harmonies

In Suno and Udio, ( ) means backing vocals / harmonies. Don't use them for asides, stage directions, or non-vocal notes — the AI will sing them.

Skipping the [Outro] tag

The #1 cause of songs that sound abruptly cut off. Always close your song structure with [Outro], even if it's just the tag with no lyrics.

Mixing incompatible tag types

Don't use [Verse] and [Verse 1] interchangeably. Be consistent — either number all verses or none. Mixing formats confuses the structural detection.

Creating mega-sections

A single [Verse] with 12 lines is a recipe for trouble. Break long blocks into multiple tagged sections. The AI needs frequent structural markers.
